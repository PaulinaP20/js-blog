    How to start running?

    To start running you should make sure you have a good pair of running shoes to support your feet. Begin with a light warm-up, like stretching or brisk walking, for about 5-10 minutues to get your muscles ready. When you start running, go at a comfortable pace, gradually inreacing the time or distance over time.

    Focus on your form by keeping your posture upright and your strides relaxed. It's imporatnt to run consistently, airming for 2 or 3 time a week, and gradually increase your intensity. The first time I propose go run for 20-30 minutes. It's best not to push yourself too hard. YOU DONT BE TIRED AFTER RUN (you will quickly become discourange). The key is consistency and making it enjoyable, rather than exhausting yourself at the beginning.

    After each run, cool down by walking and stretching to help your muscles recover. Starting slow and staying consistent will help you build endurance and avoid injury.

    What to eat?

    The successfully finish marathon, your nutrition strategy is key. Leading up to the race, the focus should be on eating plenty of carbohydrates to build up glycogen stores, which will provide energy throughtout the race.

    On the day of the race, it's imporatnt to have a light breakfast 2-3 hours before the race. As you run, your body will burn thought its glycogen store. This is why it's crusial to keep refueling during the race. Hydrate regularly, especially at air stations and consider drinking an electrolyte beverage to replace the salts lost in sweat (energy gels or chews)

    By fucing on carbs, hydration and refueling at the right times, you'll have the enegy you need to not only finish the marathon but finish strong.

    Running injuries and prevention

    Running injuries are common, but many can be prevented with proper training, technique and revocery.

    Prevent tips:

    • Warmp up and cool down - always start with dynamic stretches to warp up and finish static streches to cool down.
    • Gradual increase - increase your mileage and intensity slowly, following the '10% rule' ( dont increase by more than 10% each week)
    • Proper footwear - wear well-fitted shoes designes for you foot type and replace them every 200-500 miles.
    • Cross training - include other forms of exercise ( cycling, swimming, strenght) to avoid overuse injuries.
    • Strengthen muscles - focus on strengthening key areas like the core, hips and legs to impove stability and prevent imbalances.
    • Rest and recover - take rest days to allow your body to recover and avoid burnout. Listen to your body and don't ignore pain.

    How to choose running shoes?

    It's imporant to consider your foot type, running style and proper fit. First, identify your foot type - if your foot rolls inward too much (overpronation) you'll need stability or motion control shoes. It it rolls too little (underpronation or supination) you'll want shoes with extra cushioning. For neutral feet, a neutral shoes work well.

    Second, think about where you'll be running. If you're mostly on pavement, opt for road shoes that provide cushioning and support. For trail running, look for shoes with a more durable, rugged tread designed to handle off-road terrain.

    Testing shoes by running in them and considering comfort is key - good shoes should feel like an extension your body, supporting you without cousing discomfort.

    Streagth training for runners

    Strenght training is key for runners to improve performance, prevent injuries and build endurance. Focusing on the core helps with stability and posture, while strengthening legs and glutes improves running power and reduces joint stress.

    Exercise like squats, lunges and planks target these areas, while upper body exercise like push-ups and rows anhance posture and arm swing.

    Aim for 2-3 strength training sessions per week, ideally on non-running days of after short runs. By including strength training, you'll enhance your running efficiency and reduce risk of injury.

    Mental preparation for a marathon

    In my opinion mental is the most important thing in running. 60% of your success is in mind and 40% in your body.

    Set a clear goal, whether it's finishing, echieving a personal best or just enjoying the expercience. Break the race into smaller segment to avoid feeling overwhelmed. Visualize crossing the finish line and feeling strong. During your training, practice when you're tired or in less-than-ideal conditions. This will help you get used to pushing throught mental barriers on race day. Stay positive and have strategy for this bad moments like repeating a motivating mantra or focusing on your breathing.

    On race day, expect difficult moments and prepare for them. Have a plan for staying focused and managing discomfort, whetever it's throught pacing, hydration or mental distractions. Trust yourself and remember that the mental chellenge is just a part of the marathon experience.

    Running in any weather

    Running in any weather can be challenging but with the right approach, it can also be rewarding.

    • Hot weather: Stay hydrated, wear lightweight, moisture-wicking clothes and run during cooler parts of the day, like early morning or evening. Use sunscreen and take breaks if needed to prevent overheating.
    • Cold weather: dress in layers to stay warm with moisture-wicking base layer and an insulating layer on top. Avoid cotton, as it raps sweat. Be mindful of frostbite risk and protect extremities (hands, feet, ears).
    • Rainy weather: Wear a lightweight, waterproff jacket to stay dry and Embrace the rain as a fun challenge - you don't know what the weather will be like on day of the marathon.
    • Windy weather: wear a windproff jacket and face the wind head-on on the first half of your run so you'll have a tailwind on the way back. Adjust your pace as needed to handle resistance.

    Running in any weather requires smart preparation, so you can enjouy your runs safely, no matter the conditions.

    Running myths

    Here are some common running myths:

    • Running every dat is necessary: Rest is essential for recovery. Overtraining can lead to burnout and injury, so include rest days or cross-training.
    • Running is bad for knees: Moderate running with proper form strengthens knee muscles and helps prevent joint issues.
    • You need to stretch before runnning: Static stretching before a run can descrease performance and increase injury risk. Use dynamic streches before and static after.
    • Card loading before every run: Only long-distance runners need to focus on carb-loading. For shorter runs, a balanced meal beforehand is sufficient.
    • You need to run fast: Slower, consistent runs are just as effective for building endurance, improving health and preventing injuries "to run fast, you have to run slow".
